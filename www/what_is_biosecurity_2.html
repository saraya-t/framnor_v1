External Biosecurity

Location of the farm

An adequate site for the implementation of an aquaculture farm should provide the necessary conditions for the growth and welfare of the animals (Anon 2005). Several factors need to be taken in consideration during site selection, for instance: water supply, hydrography, depth, temperature, oxygen profile, presence of susceptible animals, predators and tidal flow (Jarp et al. 1993; Blaylock and Whelan 2004; Hinrichsen 2007). The selected site should also provide adequate shelter from adverse weather and sea conditions (Anon 2005).

The occurrence of disease is one of the main problems that arise from inappropriate spatial arrangement and site selection in aquaculture as water is a pathway for the transmission of pathogenic agents between hydrodynamically connected farms (Salama and Murray 2011). Farm proximity has been identified as a risk factor in the spread of infectious pathogens causative of important diseases to the industry, for example, Infectious salmon anemia (ISA) (McClure et al. 2005; Gustafson et al. 2007; Aldrin et al. 2010) and pancreas disease (Viljugrein et al. 2009; Aldrin et al. 2010).

Having this in mind, the Norwegian Food Safety Authority enforces 5 km restriction zone and a 10 km observation zone around ISA infected farms to help control the spread of the disease (Aldrin et al. 2011)

With this, and as in most cases it is impossible to move the farm, site selection and spatial arrangement should be carefully planned beforehand.

Introduction of eggs

The introduction of live biological material, such as eggs, into a farm constitutes a high biosecurity risk (Voice over). Fish eggs may harbour pathogenic agents and may act as an important pathway for disease transmission (Ekman et al. 1999; Peeler et al. 2011)

With this, in hatcheries, egg disinfection is an essential tool used for mortality and disease mitigation (De Swaef et al. 2016), preventing the spread of pathogenic agents between facilities, and is fundamental to minimize the risk of disease transfer through trade (OIE 2018e). In fact, in Norway, egg disinfection after fertilization is mandatory by law (Lillehaug et al. 2015).

While salmonids eggs can be disinfected with a variety of chemical agents, the use of iodophors is the most common practice (OIE 2018e).

While these disinfectants are effective in the disinfection of egg surface and reproductive fluids, they will not prevent vertical transmission, and thus, broodstock should be tested for pathogens that can be transmitted inside the eggs (Lillehaug et al. 2015)

Introduction of live fish

The introduction of live fish into the farm can inadvertently introduce disease through the fish itself and/or transport vehicles (Dvorak 2009). To prevent this, fish should be purchased from trusted suppliers and the health status of the population, from which the animal are prevenient, should be controlled before and after the introduction of the animals. The new animals should be accompanied by health certificates regarding reporting on health control program followed by fish health personnel, cases of diasease outbreak, vaccination preformed, results from diseases surveillance and test screening (Lillehaug et al. 2015). The newly arrived animals should pass through a quarantine period to minimize the risk of disease introduction to the aquatic system and to protect disease transmission to the resident fish population (Noga 2010). This quarantine should occur in dedicated areas/tanks. The areas, water and all the equipment used should be regularly cleaned, disinfected and not shared with the existing fish stock (Dvorak 2009).

Taking in mind the risk of disease introduction due to the introduction of new fish into the farm the number of introductions should be kept to a minimum.

Feed and water supply

Water source

A farm´s water source has significant influence on fish health and constitutes one of the greatest risks for introduction pathogens into a farm. Water is considered as a non-living reservoir, where pathogens can survive and thrive (Chérif and Breton 2020) and so a pathogen-free water source is imperative for a successful production (Kasai et al. 2002).

The biosecurity risks associated with the water source depends on the existence of fish populations in the water source and their health status (SCAAH 2016). As such, ground water, that does not contain aquatic animal populations, represents a lower risk, while surface water, that usually come from coastal waters or rivers, constitutes a higher risk (Kasai et al. 2002).

In open production systems, net pens or ponds, that are supplied with water directly from the surface source, water supply can be little controlled. When possible, water treatment should be employed to reduce the risk (Hasimuna et al. 2020).

Water disinfection with UV radiation and ozone are commonly used methods (Kasai et al. 2002), but, as organic mater inhibits oxidative disinfection and suspended solids reduce the efficacy of UV treatment, suspended solids should be removed beforehand, by filtration or settlement of suspended materials (OIE 2018b)

Wastewater

Fish farming production generates wastewater. This wastewater composition is directly associated with the nature and quantity of the feed provided to the fish and with the type of system in operation (Siddiqui 2003). Farms should ensure that this water is clean and free of pathogens before discharge, to prevent negative impacts, such as disease spread, to the surrounding environment (Diana 2009; SCAAH 2016). The same methods used to treat water source can be used to disinfect wastewater, and so prevent the spread of disease from one farm to another.

Feed supply

Feed does not constitute a high risk for disease introduction, as commercial dry pellets, usually used to feed fish, are not normally a source of infectious disease, due to the heat treatment received during the production process (Lillehaug et al. 2015). However, trash, frozen or live food, for example rotifers and artemia, can function as pathways for disease introduction (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012)

With this, manufactured feeds should be used in preference to live or unprocessed feeds, rotifers and artemia should be disinfected and all feed should be tested for infectious pathogens before feeding (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012, SCAAH 2016)

Automatic feeder

Biovectors

Predators can cause damage to the fish or to the farm facilities directly, indirectly or both. Direct damage occurs when fish are seriously injured or killed, resulting in production loss. There are several ways in which predators can cause indirect damage, for example: wounding fish non-lethally, being a source of chronic stress, transmission of infectious pathogens (e.g. bacteria, viruses, and parasites) to the reared stock and causing damages to the animal enclosure through which fish can escape (Bevan et al. 2002).

Piscivorous animals are attracted to aquaculture facilities due to the high fish density, shallow water, in some aquaculture systems, and similar size prey, supplying them with easy access to food (Manikowska-Ślepowrońska et al. 2016). There are several measures available to prevent birds from entering aquaculture facilities and to reduce the damage they cause (Bevan et al. 2002).

The total restriction of birds’ access to the facilities through total exclusion techniques is the only completely effective method for eradicating bird predation and the same is true for mammal predators.

Personnel and visitors

Humans, for example farm personnel and visitors, can serve as mechanical vectors for the transmission of infectious pathogens, if they have contact with infected and susceptible animals. For example, in Jarp et al. 1993, work personnel is identified as a risk factor for the spread of Aeromonas salmonicida subsp. salmonicida in Norwegian freshwater hatcheries.

Therefore, farm personnel and visitors should follow biosecurity measures to prevent the introduction and spread of disease into and within the farm (Dvorak 2009).

To prevent the transmission of infectious diseases between farms, the facility´s staff should work exclusively for one farm and should not visit other sites, as they may carry infectious material. Employees should wear clean clothing, coveralls, and footwear during work and visitors can use disposable coats. When entering and exiting production areas employees and visitors should wash or sanitize their hands and clean and disinfect their footwear, usually resorting to footbaths (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012; Lillehaug et al. 2015; OIE 2018b). Access to the farm should be restricted to working personnel, and if access is allowed to visitors, they should be followed by a staff member to enforce the implementation of biosecurity measures (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012; Lillehaug et al. 2015). These visitors should not be allowed to enter the farm after visiting another aquaculture facility and they should sign a document either confirming that they have not enterer another facility or to provide details regarding previous visits (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012)

Use of divers

In a similar way to other farm employers, divers should follow the biosecurity measures enforced by the facility as, due to the inevitable contact they and their equipment will have with contaminated material, they may pose as a pathway to disease introduction and spread (Lillehaug et al. 2015)

As such, farms should own diving equipment for their exclusive use, and diving equipment should be cleaned and disinfected before and after the diving takes place (Georgiades et al. 2016). The disinfection of this type of equipment may require special attention as they are difficult to disinfect, can be moved from farm to farm and are vulnerable to chemical corrosion (OIE 2018b). These operations should be documented and farms should check that the equipment was cleaned and disinfected before and after the dive (Georgiades et al. 2016).

Equipment and vehicles for transport of live fish, feed and waste

Equipment and vehicles used in aquaculture facilities can function as fomites, inanimate objects that can transmit diseases, facilitating the introduction and spread of disease into and within the farm.

For this, it is recommended that equipment should only be owned and used by one facility, thus eliminating the possibility of disease spread (Lillehaug et al. 2015). However, when this is not possible, and equipment is used at different sites, they should be cleaned and disinfected before entering or leaving the facility. Disinfection procedures should include a thoroughly mechanical cleaning, equipment should be washed with high-pressure jets, and suffer a final disinfection using different varieties of disinfectants. The equipment material should be considered when choosing the disinfectant (Håstein 2000).

Regarding vehicles, the likelihood of contamination is determined by their use (e.g. transportation of dead animals, live animals, harvested animals) (OIE 2018b). Vehicles should also only be used in one facility, as vehicles usually travel from farm to farm increasing the risk of contamination. As such, all farms should enforce the cleaning and disinfection of all potentially contaminated internal and external surfaces, of both land-based and sea-based transporters.

Internal Biosecurity

Vaccination

Vaccination is an important tool used to prevent and control a variety of bacterial and viral disease in aquaculture (Ma et al. 2019). Vaccines are used in almost all animal production industries and should be a complement, and not a substitute, to the implementation of health management measures (Anon 2005).

The importance of vaccination is such that , according to Robertsen (2011), Atlantic salmon farming would be impossible without vaccination against vibriosis, cold water vibriosis and furunculosis, and it has be showed that, in farms where fish were vaccinated against pancreas disease, the odds for a PD outbreak were 3 times lower, and that vaccination will also reduce mortality and the number of fish discarded (Jensen et al. 2012).

Vaccination also contributes to a reduction of the use of antimicrobials, thus diminishing the risk of selection pressure leading to antimicrobial resistance (Rodgers and Furones 2009). For example, in Norway, the use of antibacterial drugs has reduced drastically and vaccination practices are one of the main factors for this decrease (Midtlyng et al. 2011)

Disease Management

In aquaculture facilities, monitoring has an essential role in the early identification, isolation and treatment of a health problem. Preferably, the animals should be observed daily for any signs of illness or behaviour modification by employees who can recognize signs of fish illness (Francis-Floyd 1990; Bebak 1998). To ensure the early identification and consequentially respond rapidly to the presence of disease, animals showing signs of distress or dying fish should be submitted for diagnostic testing or necropsy (Klinger and Francis-Floyd 2001). Diseased animals should be quickly removed and placed in an isolated area, away from the rest of the stock. This area should have dedicated equipment, water sources, and personnel working in this section should not have contact with the rest of the stock. Care and treatment of diseased fish should be handled after the healthy fish, or by staff dedicated exclusively to the sick fish. These courses of action prevent the transmission of infectious diseases between sick and healthy animals (Dvorak 2009).

Diagnostic testing of fish stock is an important tool to prevent the introduction (e.g. testing before introducing new fish into the farm), spread (fish health should be evaluated regularly), and transmission (e.g. before sending fish to other facilities) of pathogenic diseases into, within and between farms. Surveillance, the systematic series of investigations of a given population of aquatic animals to detect the occurrence of disease for control purposes, is performed by aquaculture farms to demonstrate the absence of disease, to identify events that need to be notified, to determine the occurrence or distribution of endemic diseases, to provide information for disease control programs and to gather information on disease occurrence to be used by training partners (OIE 2018a)

However, there is always the possibility that, even when following all recommended management and biosecurity measures, a farm can become affected by an infectious disease. As such, farms should develop contingency plans for possible disease outbreaks (Palić and Scarfe 2018). Contingency plans should also be developed for the eventuality of environmental pollution and equipment failure.

Rearing management

Farmed animals are susceptible to a larger range of infectious diseases. The outcome of infection and the maintenance of the pathogen in an animal population depends on three factors:

  • The host;
  • The environment;
  • The pathogen

As such, disturbances in one of these factors, for example, environmental alterations, may lead to infection and disease (Dunowska 2018)

Non-infectious diseases can also have damaging effects on the industry. These diseases are generally caused by factors, such as poor water quality, inappropriate nutrition, environmental degradation, among others (Subasinghe 2014).

Water quality

Optimum water quality is crucial for the production of farmed fish, as fluctuations or the inadequate maintenance of important parameters, for example, oxygen, ammonia, nitrate and pH, may compromise fish health (Håstein 2004; Dvorak 2009). As such, water parameters should be regularly checked and regulated to provide fish the best rearing conditions.

Stocking density

Stocking density is an important variable in aquaculture and should be adjusted according with the biological and behavioural needs of the fish having regard to the environmental conditions (e.g. water quality) (CoGP 2015). Excessive stocking density can originate stress and one consequence to their immune function, when fish are chronically exposed to adverse conditions, is increased vulnerability to disease (FBSI 2002). High stocking density also increases fish-to-fish contact, which in turn can result in an increased rate of pathogen spreading (Anon 2005). With this, stocking density should be maintained optimal to decrease fish susceptibility to infection.

Year class separation

The all-in all-out principle is one of the most important biosecurity measures, preventing the spread of infectious disease from one production cycle to the next. In this practice, fish with different year classes (cohorts) are kept separately. All fish in the same cohort should be harvested or removed at the same time (Håstein 2000; Blaylock and Whelan 2004).

Feed management

While feed is not usually a vector for disease introduction and spread, it might still constitute a risk if proper biosecurity measures are not followed.

Dry feeds should be kept in cool and dry areas to prevent spoilage, contamination and mould growth and moist feed should be accordingly refrigerated in a safe storage area (according to the manufacturer’s instructions) (FAO and WHO 2009).

Improperly stored feed may contain pathogenic bacteria or mycotoxins, dangerous chemicals produced by the growth of some fungi in the feed, and due to the degradation of macro and micronutrients, reduced nutrition value. If improperly stored, feed may also become exposed to pests increasing the risk of contamination (Yanong and Erlacher-Reid 2012).

Considering this, to guarantee feed safety, farms should take precautions, such as, storing the food in cool and dry conditions, conduct frequent inspections of the feed and store it in areas where pests cannot access.

Waste management

In aquaculture farms, fish mortalities is a natural occurrence. Animals deaths may be caused by, disease, age, mismanagement, predation, congenital defects and several other reasons (NZ King Salmon 2011; EPA SA 2019).

Dead animals, and their products, should be appropriately and safely disposed and should be removed as soon as possible to prevent disease transmission to the remaining fish stock and for the early identification of a health problem (Håstein 2000; Klinger and Francis-Floyd 2001).

Containers used to store dead fish and fish waste should be leak-proof and stored in dedicated areas, located away from production sites and water bodies to minimize the risk of spread of pathogenic agents. Farms should also ensure that predatory animals or scavengers cannot reach the dead animals as they could spread infectious materials (OIE 2018d)

Harvesting

Dead fish, fish blood and other body fluids are sources of infectious agents. As such, the harvest process may lead to the spread of a disease within and between farms (Munro et al. 2003; Anon 2005).

To reduce this risk, the resulting products of the harvest, for example, blood and effluents, should be contained and disinfected, and harvesting material, that can function as fomites, should be checked for damages, cleaned and disinfected between harvests (CoGP 2015).

Equipment, Cleaning, and Disinfectant onsite

As equipment used in aquaculture productions can function as fomites it is recommended that it should only be owned and used by one facility (Lillehaug et al. 2015). Following this measures is of course more important when the farm is experiencing a disease outbreak. In that instance, the equipment should only be used for the diseased parts of the facility.

Taking this in consideration, cleaning and disinfection are essential procedures to reduce the risk of disease transmission and for disease control (RUMA 2006). It usually involves both a physical and a chemical procedure that are applied to the target (Georgiades et al. 2006).

An effective cleaning and disinfection protocol should include:

  • Initial cleaning, to remove organic material
  • Washing with detergent, to help break down biofilms or any adherent organic material
  • Rinsing, to remove any chemical residues that may counteract the efficiency of the disinfectants
  • Drying, to prevent the dilution of the disinfectant that will be applied
  • Inspection of the cleaned equipment, to ensure removal of all material
  • Application of disinfectants, to inactivate pathogenic agents
  • Removal or inactivation of disinfectants, to avoid their contact with the animals, corrosion and consequences to the environment.

Bisecurity program and record keeping

Biosecurity progoram

To ensure the effectiveness of a biosecurity program, all farm personnel should be aware of the risks and costs that may arise if the required biosecurity measures are not implemented, and of the benefits to be gained if they follow the developed biosecurity plan. For this, training programs, on the application of biosecurity and health management measures, are essential to ensure the education of the facility personnel. This training should be ongoing, and documentation of the training should be recorded (Perera et al. 2008; Georgiades et al. 2016). Farms should also assemble a manual of standard procedures (SOPs) to supply a set of standard rules for biosecurity measures and disease monitoring (Smith 1998).

Record keeping

Record keeping is imperative to the success of biosecurity programmes as it provides accurate historical information regarding the health status, weight gains, feed consumption, vaccinations or treatments, and management practices of the facility (Smith 1998). Farm record analysis is a great tool used in disease management and, along with periodic farm visits and reviews by attending veterinarians, are the basis for auditing and certification (Palić and Scarfe 2018).
